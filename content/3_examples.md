**Here are a few examples of scientific work** that we have collaborated on in the CDIL to help you think about ways you can reconsider and reframe your own work. 

{% include gallery-figure.html img="ds_science_07.jpg" alt="Index card with a photograph of a moth with ornately patterned wings." caption="Alfalfa looper moth from the Plant, Soil, and Entomological Sciences Records collection, link below." width="100%" %}

The [Plant, Soil, and Entomological Sciences Records collection](https://www.lib.uidaho.edu/digital/pses/) was created by Gary Strong Fellowship recipient Kate Morrison "to highlight the entomological work of the College of Agricultural and Life Sciences at the University of Idaho between 1935-1966." The digital approach was created to "serve as important data banks for research into documenting speciesâ€™ population and diversity, improving public health, enhancing agricultural practices, ensuring food security, and monitoring global changes."

{% include gallery-figure.html img="ds_science_08.png" alt="Visualization of multi-colored bands connecting emotions on the left side with interviewee names on the right." caption="Visualization from The Chestnut Collective, link below." width="100%" %}

[The Chestnut Collective](https://thecdil.github.io/chestnutcollective/) is another example of digital scholarship applied to multidisciplinary scientific research. Created bby Nick Koenig for a CDIL Graduate Fellowship, the project is a "sociocultural and sociopolitical research project tracking the blight and subsequent loss of the American Chestnut on Appalachian families and communities." This project implements Oral History as Data, another offshoot of CollectionBuilder, to enrich interview recordings with subject and tag data, which were various emotional descriptions. This visualization on the landing page allows visitors to the site to explore recordings by interviewee or these tagged emotions. 

{% include gallery-figure.html img="ds_science_02.jpg" alt="Bowl against a black background with blue cobalt illustrations of bats and a swan." caption="Item from the Historical Japanese Ceramic Comparative Collection, link below." width="100%" %}

The [Historical Japanese Ceramic Comparative Collection](https://www.lib.uidaho.edu/digital/hjccc/) is an example of digital scholarship in historical archaeology by Ph.D. Candidate Renae Campbell as part of her 2018 CDIL Digital Scholarship Fellowship. This collection features multiple "child" items per page, allowing researchers to engage with the artifacts remotely but thoroughly. There is also quite a bit of customized metadata, allowing visitors in the archaeological field to make chemical connections around the type of materials, decoration method and the pigments that were applied to the objects. This work is also complimented by with a glossary with kanji translations and a helpful visualized directory, where readers can explore the collection by vessel type.
